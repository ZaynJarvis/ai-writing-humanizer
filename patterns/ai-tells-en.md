# English AI-Writing Tells and Fixes

Use this catalog as a checklist when editing English prose. Each entry shows the tell, why it sounds mechanical, and the fix.

## Structural tells

### 1. Paragraph symmetry
- **Tell:** Every paragraph is the same length or starts the same way.
- **Fix:** Vary paragraph length. Let some paragraphs be one sentence. Let others run longer if the idea demands it.

### 2. List addiction
- **Tell:** The text turns every idea into a numbered or bulleted list.
- **Fix:** Convert lists back into flowing prose where the relationships between ideas matter more than the items themselves.

### 3. Transition formula
- **Tell:** Overuse of "firstly/secondly/finally", "moreover", "furthermore", "in conclusion", "in summary".
- **Fix:** Use transitions that reveal the actual relationship: "but", "so", "because", "instead", "meanwhile", "then".

### 4. Section bloat
- **Tell:** Every section has an explicit topic sentence, three supporting points, and a summary sentence.
- **Fix:** Remove the summary sentence if the next paragraph already follows. Drop the topic sentence if the heading already announces the topic.

### 5. Hook-template fatigue
- **Tell:** Opens with "In today's world...", "In the era of...", "Have you ever wondered...", or a dictionary definition.
- **Fix:** Start with a specific scene, a concrete example, a disagreement, or a direct claim.

## Lexical tells

### 6. AI buzzwords
- **Tell:** "leverage", "delve", "tapestry", "landscape", "journey", "robust", "seamless", "multifaceted", "holistic", "utilize".
- **Fix:** Replace with plain verbs: use, look into, explore, strong, smooth, many-sided, whole, use.

### 7. Hedge overload
- **Tell:** Repeated phrases like "it is important to note", "it should be considered", "arguably", "one could argue".
- **Fix:** State the claim directly. Keep hedges only when genuine uncertainty is the point.

### 8. Empty intensifiers
- **Tell:** "very", "extremely", "highly", "significantly" without concrete backing.
- **Fix:** Cut the intensifier or replace it with a specific measurement or comparison.

### 9. Sycophantic polish
- **Tell:** "I hope this helps", "I'm excited to", "feel free to", "please don't hesitate to" in contexts where a human would be more direct.
- **Fix:** Be polite but brief. "Let me know if you need more." instead of "Please do not hesitate to reach out."

### 10. Faux-certainty endings
- **Tell:** "In summary, X is a powerful tool that will transform Y."
- **Fix:** End with the real implication, a question, or a caveat. Avoid grand claims that the text did not earn.

## Syntactic tells

### 11. Copula avoidance
- **Tell:** Every sentence avoids "is/are" by using "represents", "constitutes", "serves as", "functions as".
- **Fix:** Use "is" when it is the clearest verb. Do not force nominalizations for variety.

### 12. Parallelism excess
- **Tell:** "not only X but also Y", "either X or Y", "both X and Y" appear repeatedly.
- **Fix:** Rewrite most of them as plain compound or complex sentences.

### 13. Tricolon addiction
- **Tell:** "X, Y, and Z" triplets everywhere.
- **Fix:** Use one triplet for emphasis; otherwise list two items or weave them into prose.

### 14. Long appositive chains
- **Tell:** "a leading, innovative, and comprehensive solution".
- **Fix:** Keep one or two adjectives. Move the rest into a separate sentence or cut them.

### 15. Passive voice default
- **Tell:** Passive constructions used when active would be clearer.
- **Fix:** Name the actor. "The team built X" instead of "X was built by the team."

## Punctuation and formatting tells

### 16. Em dash overuse
- **Tell:** Em dashes used as a stylistic crutch in every paragraph.
- **Fix:** Use parentheses, commas, or a separate sentence.

### 17. Bold overuse
- **Tell:** Bolding every other phrase.
- **Fix:** Bold only the one or two things a reader must remember.

### 18. Quote-hallucination block
- **Tell:** A standalone inspirational quote that no one asked for.
- **Fix:** Remove it unless the quote is genuinely cited and relevant.

### 19. Uniform sentence length
- **Tell:** All sentences are 15–25 words.
- **Fix:** Insert a short sentence. Break a long sentence into two. Allow fragments in conversational register.

## Voice and register tells

### 20. No stance
- **Tell:** The text reads like a committee summary; no opinion, no friction, no point of view.
- **Fix:** State what the author actually thinks. Include a specific preference, concern, or disagreement.

### 21. Reader-moralizing
- **Tell:** "remember", "always", "never" used to lecture the reader.
- **Fix:** Convert imperatives into observations or conditional advice.

### 22. Emotion-by-label
- **Tell:** Saying something is "exciting" or "shocking" instead of showing it.
- **Fix:** Show the concrete detail that makes it exciting or shocking.

### 23. Generic you
- **Tell:** "you may find that..." directed at an abstract reader.
- **Fix:** Address a specific reader or use "we" if the author and reader share the context.

### 24. Knowledge-cutoff disclaimer
- **Tell:** "as of my last update", "my knowledge cutoff is...".
- **Fix:** Remove meta-AI references entirely. If dates matter, state the date or leave the statement timeless.

### 25. Over-explaining
- **Tell:** Defining obvious terms or stating intent the reader can already infer.
- **Fix:** Cut the explanation. Trust the reader.
